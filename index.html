When the world got scary, Bitcoin moved like a stock, not like gold.

On 22 June 2025, markets fixed their eyes on a strip of water barely 33 kilometres wide. After American airstrikes on Iran, Tehran's parliament voted to close the Strait of Hormuz, the channel that carries close to a fifth of the world's seaborne oil. If Bitcoin were truly digital gold, this was its moment to prove it.

It did the opposite. Over the shock, Bitcoin tracked the S&P 500 almost four times more closely than it tracked gold. Below is the data, and exactly how I tested it.

I built this as a companion to my Master's thesis on institutional DeFi risk, turning a strategy question into a measurable, reproducible analysis.

Method & limitations

Approach

  • Daily prices pulled from the Yahoo Finance API, then cleaned and merged in PowerShell.
  • Series aligned on common trading days, because Bitcoin trades every day while gold and oil do not.
  • Pearson correlation computed on daily returns rather than raw prices, which avoids spurious trend correlation.
  • The window split into before, during and after the event, plus a 30-day rolling correlation.

Intellectual honesty

  • A single event proves nothing statistically. This is a case study, not a law.
  • The fast de-escalation, since Hormuz never actually closed, shortened the shock to a nine-day window.
  • The finding still matches the literature: the BTC and equity correlation rises in periods of stress.
  • Next step: replicate the test across other shocks, such as COVID in 2020 and the 2023 banking crisis, to generalize.

Bottom line

When fear struck, Bitcoin behaved like a risk-on asset, not a store of value. During the Hormuz shock it was almost four times more correlated to the S&P 500 than to gold. It fell with markets and bounced back with them. Digital gold, for now, it is not.
